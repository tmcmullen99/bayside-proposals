The problem

Every Bayside designer runs
their own sales process.

And it's costing us deals.

  • Each designer's proposal format is different — emailed PDFs, paper printouts, phone-only conversations
  • No visibility once the proposal goes out the door
  • Follow-up timing is guessed, not informed
  • Substitutions and questions live in scattered channels — texts, emails, voicemails
  • Leadership sees closed deals at month-end. Stalled deals go silent

Deals don't usually die because Bayside lost a price war.
They die because the homeowner went quiet and no one knew when to re-engage.

Bayside has a strong design team. The CRAFT is there. But the SYSTEM underneath the craft is inconsistent designer-to-designer. Each designer figures out their own way to follow up, their own format, their own cadence. Two homeowners shopping with two Bayside designers get two completely different experiences. The most expensive part of this isn't aesthetic. It's: deals don't usually die because Bayside lost a price war. They die because the homeowner went quiet and no one knew when to re-engage at the right moment. That's the problem I built BPB to solve.

10 / 15 The Deal
How the deal works

Two sides, aligned incentives

What McMullen does

  • Provides and maintains the platform
  • Provides 30-min training for each designer
  • Invoices monthly for closed deals
  • Provides full attribution reports
  • Supports designers with 4-hour SLA
  • Builds new platform features

What Bayside does

  • Uses BPB for proposals
  • Requires new designers to certify
  • Pays monthly invoices Net-15
  • May allocate 50% of 1% to designer of record
  • Reports closed deals in the platform
  • Owns all their homeowner and deal data

The designer split is entirely Bayside's call. Pass all of it, half of it, or none of it — McMullen's fee is calculated on the gross contract value either way.

Two columns. McMullen runs the platform, provides training, invoices monthly with full attribution data. Bayside uses it for proposals, requires new hires to certify, and pays after close. One important option: Bayside can pass up to half of the 1% directly to the designer who did the work. So if you split it 50/50, the designer earns 0.5% on every closed BPB deal — a meaningful comp bump that incentivizes them to actually use the system. The split is entirely Bayside's call. You could keep all of it, or pass none of it, or do something in between. McMullen doesn't care about that part.

Concrete math. On a $50K Bayside install, the 1% fee is $500 — or $250 if you split 50/50 with the designer. For comparison: per-user SaaS platforms like Salesforce or HubSpot would run Bayside six grand a year for 5 designers, paid whether or not you close any deals. BPB at 50 deals a year — which would be a strong year — would be $12,500 with the designer split, but every dollar of that is paid AFTER you've already collected the revenue. Bottom line: this works out to 0.5% of pipeline revenue, and only the pipeline revenue that actually closes. The 'cost' of the platform pays for itself with any single recovered deal that would have otherwise gone silent.
